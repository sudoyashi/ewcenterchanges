Global Issues Local Trends – DRAFT by Josh

Thailand is a useful case study for understanding trends that are sweeping the globe. These trends range from economic shifts and demographic changes—including urban stresses driven by internal as well as cross-border migrations—to recent democratic regression and crackdowns on journalists and artists by authoritarian leaders.

China’s Rise and Its Role in Thailand and Southeast Asia

Another lens through which we will examine Thailand is in the context of China. Through trade and commercial investments (often led by the region’s top business leaders with Chinese ancestry), China looms large in the development plans of Thailand and countries across Southeast Asia. At the same time, China’s assertions of sovereignty on issues such as the South China Sea provoke regional and international tensions.

Tensions Over Migration, Ethnicity and Religion

Moreover, in Thailand, as in other parts of Southeast Asia, forces of ethnicity and religion have erupted in recent years, as have rising anti-migrant sentiments, influencing domestic and regional politics. In a country that relies on more than 3 million migrant workers, the vast majority from neighboring Myanmar (also known as Burma), how Thailand embraces diversity and multiculturalism will be critical to addressing inter-ethnic tensions within and beyond its borders. In addition to learning about the plight of migrant workers in Bangkok, we will visit a Thai-Burmese border area to get an up-close look at the large-scale issues and daily challenges facing both Burmese migrants and stateless ethnic minorities, many of whom were born in Thailand.

Climate Change and Impacts

Importantly, Thailand and particularly its capital city of Bangkok, is increasingly confronted with impacts of climate change—from severe weather and sea level rise to flooding. To better understand climate risks and improve resilience, Thailand has embarked on various initiatives and projects, from mapping out an extensive water-management plan to setting up flood prevention and response centers in target areas. Chulalongkorn University Centenary Park (CU Park for short) is one such project that we will visit. Built on an 11-acre green space that can hold up to million gallons of water, the park helps retain and redirect water that would otherwise flow into city streets and flood surrounding areas.
